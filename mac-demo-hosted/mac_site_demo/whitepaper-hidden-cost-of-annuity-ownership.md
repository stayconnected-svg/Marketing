## Introduction

If you own a variable or indexed annuity, there's a good chance you're paying more than you realize. Not because anyone is cheating you — but because the fee structure of most annuity contracts is designed to be difficult to total up.

After spending 20 years as an annuity wholesaler representing major insurance carriers across eight states, I've seen firsthand how these contracts are built. The fees aren't hidden in the illegal sense — they're disclosed in your prospectus and contract. But they're spread across so many layers that most owners never calculate the total.

This guide will show you exactly where to look, how to add it up, and what questions to ask once you know your number.

## The 6 Fee Layers Inside Most Variable Annuity Contracts

Most annuity owners know they pay *something*. Very few know they're paying in six different places simultaneously.

### Layer 1: Mortality & Expense Risk Charge (M&E)

**What it is:** A daily charge deducted from your account value that compensates the insurance company for the mortality risk they assume and their administrative expenses.

**Typical range:** 1.00% – 1.50% annually

**Where to find it:** Your prospectus, under "Annual Contract Charges" or "Insurance Charges."

**What most owners miss:** This fee is charged regardless of your contract's performance. In a year your account loses value, you still pay M&E on top of that loss.

### Layer 2: Administrative Fees

**What it is:** A flat annual charge or percentage-based fee for maintaining your contract, processing transactions, and generating statements.

**Typical range:** $30 – $50/year flat fee, OR 0.10% – 0.25% annually

**Where to find it:** Your annual statement or prospectus under "Contract Maintenance Charge."

**What most owners miss:** Some contracts waive this fee above a certain account value threshold. If your value drops below that threshold, the fee kicks back in.

### Layer 3: Sub-Account Management Fees (Fund Expenses)

**What it is:** The operating expenses of the underlying investment options (sub-accounts) you've selected within the annuity. These function like mutual fund expense ratios.

**Typical range:** 0.50% – 2.00% annually (varies widely by sub-account)

**Where to find it:** The fund fact sheets for each sub-account, or the "Investment Options" section of your prospectus.

**What most owners miss:** This is the fee layer that varies the most and is often the largest single charge. If you selected sub-accounts when you opened the contract and haven't reviewed them since, you may be in higher-cost options when lower-cost alternatives exist within the same contract.

### Layer 4: Rider Charges (Optional Benefit Fees)

**What it is:** Annual fees for optional guarantees you've added to your contract — income riders (GLWB), death benefit riders, accumulation riders, etc.

**Typical range:** 0.50% – 1.50% per rider, annually

**Where to find it:** Your contract schedule page or annual statement under "Optional Benefit Charges" or "Rider Fees."

**What most owners miss:** Rider fees are typically charged on the *benefit base*, not the account value. The benefit base can be significantly higher than your actual account value, meaning you're paying a percentage on a number larger than what you'd receive if you surrendered the contract. Additionally, some riders increase their fee percentage at each contract anniversary.

### Layer 5: Surrender Charges

**What it is:** A declining penalty charged if you withdraw more than your free withdrawal allowance or fully surrender the contract within a specified period (typically 5–8 years from purchase or last purchase payment).

**Typical range:** 7% in year 1, declining by ~1% per year to 0%

**Where to find it:** Your contract schedule page under "Surrender Charge Schedule" or "Contingent Deferred Sales Charge."

**What most owners miss:** Some contracts reset the surrender schedule when you add money or elect a new rider. You may believe you're past the surrender period based on when you originally opened the contract, but a subsequent action restarted the clock.

### Layer 6: Transfer and Transaction Fees

**What it is:** Charges for exceeding the allowed number of free transfers between sub-accounts per year, or for certain administrative transactions.

**Typical range:** $10 – $25 per transfer after the free allowance (typically 12–15 free per year)

**Where to find it:** Your prospectus under "Transfer Fees" or "Transaction Charges."

**What most owners miss:** This is typically the smallest fee layer, but it can add up if you're actively managing sub-account allocations.

## How to Calculate Your True All-In Annual Cost

Here's a simple worksheet approach. Pull out your most recent annual statement and prospectus:

**Step 1:** Find your M&E charge percentage → _____%

**Step 2:** Find your administrative fee percentage (or divide flat fee by account value) → _____%

**Step 3:** Find the weighted average expense ratio of your sub-accounts (weight each sub-account's expense ratio by what percentage of your total it represents) → _____%

**Step 4:** Add up all rider fees (check if charged on benefit base or account value — if benefit base, multiply by benefit base ÷ account value to get the effective percentage on your actual money) → _____%

**Step 5:** Total = Step 1 + Step 2 + Step 3 + Step 4 → _____%

**Step 6:** Multiply your account value by that total percentage → $_____ per year

### What's Typical?

Based on publicly available industry data:

- **Low-cost contracts:** 1.50% – 2.00% all-in
- **Average contracts:** 2.50% – 3.50% all-in
- **High-cost contracts:** 3.50% – 4.50%+ all-in

On a $500,000 annuity, the difference between 2.00% and 3.50% is **$7,500 per year** — or $75,000 over a decade — that compounds against you.

## The Rider Break-Even Formula

Income riders (GLWB, GMWB) are the most common optional benefits — and the most misunderstood. Here's how to evaluate whether yours is likely to pay for itself:

### The Question to Ask

"At what point will the total income I receive from this rider exceed what I would have received by simply withdrawing from my account value without the rider?"

### The Formula

**Break-even point (in years) = Total rider fees paid ÷ (Guaranteed annual income − Income without rider)**

If you need to live 25+ years past your activation date for the rider to break even, and you're already 68, that's a relevant data point for your decision.

### Important Considerations

- The rider provides a *floor* of income regardless of market performance. Its value is not purely mathematical — there is an insurance component.
- If markets perform well, you may never need the guarantee, and the rider cost becomes pure expense.
- If markets perform poorly or you live significantly longer than average, the rider can provide substantial value.
- This is not a recommendation to keep or drop any rider. It is a framework for understanding what you're paying for and under what circumstances you'd benefit.

## Questions to Ask Before Renewing or Surrendering

Whether you're considering keeping your current annuity, surrendering it, or exchanging it for another contract (a 1035 exchange), here are the questions that will clarify your decision:

### About Your Current Contract

1. What is my total all-in annual fee (use the worksheet above)?
2. Where am I in my surrender schedule — and what would it cost to leave today?
3. Am I using (or likely to use) every rider I'm paying for?
4. Have my sub-account options been reviewed in the last 2 years?
5. Is my beneficiary designation current and structured the way I intend?

### About Any Proposed Replacement

1. What are the total all-in fees of the proposed new contract?
2. Does the new contract have a new surrender schedule — and how long is it?
3. Am I losing any grandfathered benefits by leaving my current contract?
4. What is the tax impact of this change (if not a 1035 exchange)?
5. Who benefits more from this exchange — me, or the person recommending it?

### About Your Broader Situation

1. Does this annuity still fit my overall financial plan and timeline?
2. Have my income needs or tax situation changed since I purchased this?
3. Could my goals be met more cost-effectively through a different approach?

## What This Guide Is — and What It Isn't

**This guide is** an educational resource designed to help you understand the fee structure of annuity contracts so you can have more informed conversations with your financial professional.

**This guide is not** investment advice, a recommendation to buy or sell any product, or a suggestion that any particular annuity is appropriate or inappropriate for your situation.

Every annuity owner's circumstances are different. Fees are one factor among many — including tax treatment, income guarantees, death benefits, time horizon, and overall financial goals.

The purpose of understanding your costs is not necessarily to eliminate them. It's to ensure you're making informed decisions about what you're paying for and why.
